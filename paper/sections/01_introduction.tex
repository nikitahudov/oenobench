\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Large language models are increasingly evaluated on knowledge-intensive
benchmarks, but the most widely-used resources --- MMLU
\citep{hendrycks2021mmlu}, GPQA \citep{rein2024gpqa}, and their successors
\citep{srivastava2023bigbench, zhong2024agieval, phan2025hle} --- share
three properties that limit their usefulness as the field matures: (i)
they aggregate breadth at the expense of \emph{depth} within any single
discipline, (ii) they are typically authored or curated by a small team
using a single generation pipeline, exposing them to systematic
blind-spots and stylistic regularities that frontier models can learn to
exploit \citep{panickssery2024selfpref, zheng2023judging}, and (iii) the
questions are increasingly suspected of contamination through web-scale
pre-training corpora \citep{sainz2023contamination, magar2022contamination,
dodge2021documenting}. As models grow more capable, the marginal value of
a benchmark depends increasingly on \emph{how} it was constructed, not
only on what it contains.

We argue that domain-specialised benchmarks built around bodies of
knowledge that are \emph{externally validated by humans} --- through
certification, regulation, or peer-reviewed practice --- offer a useful
complement to broad knowledge benchmarks. Examples already exist in
medicine \citep{jin2021medqa, jin2019pubmedqa}, law
\citep{guha2023legalbench}, and finance \citep{islam2023financebench}. We
extend this line of work to a domain that has so far received little
attention from the benchmarking community, despite being unusually
well-defined: \textbf{wine}.

\paragraph{Why wine.} Wine is a multi-disciplinary subject that
integrates plant biology, geology and climate science, organic chemistry,
food microbiology, sensory neuroscience, regulatory law, and business
economics. It is one of very few domains for which (a) authoritative
governmental and inter-governmental sources publish structured taxonomic
data (e.g.\ INAO, OIV, TTB, regional consortia), (b) university research
groups (notably UC Davis, Geisenheim, Bordeaux Sciences Agro) produce
open scholarly literature, and (c) professional certification bodies
define a graded competence ladder (WSET Levels 1--4, Court of Master
Sommeliers, Institute of Masters of Wine) whose published syllabi can be
used to ground what ``expert'' should mean. Together these properties
make it possible to build a benchmark in which every question is
traceable to a verifiable source, while still spanning the
recall--reasoning--application spectrum that distinguishes a strong
taster, winemaker, or buyer from a memorised glossary.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep -1pt
  \item \textbf{A 3{,}266-question wine benchmark} covering six pillars
        (regions, varieties, viticulture, winemaking, producers,
        business), built from 38{,}104 atomic facts whose provenance is
        recorded down to the source URL and tier of authority
        (Section~\ref{sec:dataset}).
  \item \textbf{An LLM-driven, fact-grounded question-generation
        pipeline} that combines five generation strategies and five
        generator models. The LLM's role is reformatting verified facts,
        \emph{not} providing them; an explicit closed-book gate flags
        questions solvable from world knowledge alone, and a per-strategy
        quota prevents pipeline mono-culture.
  \item \textbf{A nine-agent automated quality audit}
        (Section~\ref{sec:audit}) consisting of static, statistical, and
        tri-judge agents organised into four teams, calibrated against a
        human gold sheet using Cohen's $\kappa$. The audit removed 341
        questions and re-labelled the difficulty of 1{,}259 in the final
        release. Crucially, the audit also revealed that one of its own
        agents (closed-book solvability) over-reports leakage relative
        to humans; we kept the affected questions with explicit
        disclosure rather than dropping them, and demonstrate the
        consequences in the evaluation.
  \item \textbf{A bias-aware evaluation across sixteen frontier
        configurations} (Section~\ref{sec:eval}) reporting overall
        accuracy, reasoning-mode lift, a Self-Preference Score that
        quantifies own-vs-other-generator gaps, a cost-efficiency Pareto
        analysis, and a closed-book vs.\ source-grounded contrast that
        isolates parametric memorisation from contextual reasoning.
\end{enumerate}
