\section{Applications and Future Work}
\label{sec:future}

OenoBench is a benchmark first, but the construction pipeline behind
it is intended to be reusable. We outline three threads that build on
the released artifacts and methodology.

\subsection{Closing the wine-knowledge gap in low-cost models}
The cost-vs-accuracy frontier (Section~\ref{sec:eval-cost}) shows
small open-weight models trailing proprietary frontier models by
20--30\,pp here, where the gap on broad benchmarks like MMLU is only
10--15\,pp. The 38{,}104-fact corpus is a natural \emph{supervised
fine-tuning} target via a two-stage recipe: (i) instruction-style
fine-tuning on a held-out quartile of question/source-fact pairs,
teaching source-grounded reasoning; and (ii) LoRA adaptation on the
full 38{,}104-fact corpus, exposed by the release schema as
fact-question-source triples. Both run on consumer GPUs without
proprietary weights or pre-training compute. The contextual slice
(Section~\ref{sec:eval-cb}) is the harder-to-game evaluation surface,
so measured gain is a faithful proxy for genuine wine reasoning. We
expect a Llama-3.3-70B-class adaptation to recover much of the
GPT-5-mini gap at $\approx$1\% of proprietary inference cost, opening
cost-sensitive deployment paths for sommelier-tooling and
wine-education applications.

\subsection{Case-based evaluation with industry partners}
Multiple-choice tests have ceilings: a perfect OenoBench score does
not establish that a model can act usefully in real workflows. We are
scoping an \textbf{applied case-based dataset} with industry partners
along three personas:
\begin{itemize}\itemsep -1pt
  \item \emph{Sommelier $\rightarrow$ customer service:} consumer
    pairing prompts rated by certified sommeliers on recommendation,
    justification, and substitutions.
  \item \emph{Winemaker $\rightarrow$ blending decisions:} vintage
    write-ups, lab analyses, and regulatory constraints in context;
    outputs rated on technical correctness, regulatory compliance,
    and stylistic intent.
  \item \emph{Viticulturist $\rightarrow$ vineyard decisions:}
    weather, soil/disease, canopy/yield records; canopy, irrigation,
    harvest-timing, and pest-control recommendations rated against
    decisions actually taken in the same vintage.
\end{itemize}
This requires expert annotation of free-form outputs, but the
OenoBench pipeline (multi-strategy generation, multi-agent audit,
gold-sheet calibration) and the IRR-aware human-review app
(Appendix~\ref{app:screenshots}) carry over. We treat OenoBench as
the \emph{factual-recall floor}: factual knowledge must be
near-saturated before applied reasoning can be validly measured.

\subsection{Generalising the LLM-driven scrape-to-audit pipeline}
The pipeline elements --- (i) tier-of-authority source taxonomy,
(ii) atomic-fact extraction with paraphrase enforcement,
(iii) multi-strategy multi-model generation with per-model quotas,
(iv) automated multi-agent audit calibrated against humans by
$\kappa$, and (v) closed-book pre-screen for parametric leakage ---
are domain-agnostic, implemented as separable modules. Swapping the
source-tier configuration and domain taxonomy is sufficient to
retarget. Three near-term targets share wine's structural features:
\emph{horticulture/agronomy} (regulated appellations, peer-reviewed
journals, certification ladders); \emph{regulated medicine} (clinical
guidelines and drug formularies --- the strongest tier-of-authority
structure of any field, where current benchmarks rely on board-exam
questions misaligned with practice); and \emph{financial regulation}
(FASB, IFRS, jurisdictional filings; CFA/CPA ladders). In each case
the released code reduces a months-long expert-authoring effort to a
weeks-long curation effort, given careful tier-of-authority
configuration.
