\section{Multi-Agent Quality Audit}
\label{sec:audit}

The audit pipeline runs every generated question through nine automated agents organised into four teams. Each agent emits a per-question signal in $\{\textsc{pass}, \textsc{warn}, \textsc{fail}\}$, and signals are calibrated against a human-reviewed gold sheet using Cohen's $\kappa$. Agents whose human agreement falls below a threshold are downweighted in aggregation, on the principle that an automated judge whose verdict humans cannot reproduce should not gate question admission.

Five additional agents (C1 DistractorDifficulty, B3 ParaphraseStability, B4 Ambiguity, C3 SourceSwap, D2 DedupCalibration) are implemented but \emph{escalation-gated}: they are invoked only when an upstream agent flags a corpus-level concern. This deferred-execution policy keeps the routine audit cost bounded while preserving full coverage when a defect class is suspected.

\subsection{Gold-sheet calibration}
\label{sec:gold}
A human gold sheet of 130 questions drawn from \texttt{audit\_pilot\_v13} is rated against eight rubrics: \texttt{answer\_correct}, \texttt{needs\_source}, \texttt{no\_vague\_language}, \texttt{verbatim\_copy}, \texttt{source\_faithful}, \texttt{wine\_category\_leak}, \texttt{distractors\_plausible}, and an overall pass/fail. The reviewer is the lead author (Diploma, WSET).

For each automated agent we compute Cohen's $\kappa$ against the corresponding human rubric. \textbf{Agent signals with $\kappa < 0.6$ are downweighted to advisory-only and excluded from gating decisions.} Several rubrics in the pilot exhibited $\kappa$ at or near zero: in those cases the human rating is treated as ground truth and the agent is queued for re-implementation rather than rejected outright. We view this --- the audit catching itself --- as a feature: an opaque ensemble that always agreed with the humans would be hard to trust.

\subsection{Pilot results: \texttt{audit\_pilot\_v13}}
\label{sec:pilot-results}
Across 146 questions, the nine-agent ensemble produced 561 \textsc{pass} / 125 \textsc{warn} / 66 \textsc{fail} signals. Headline findings:

\begin{itemize}\itemsep -1pt
  \item \textbf{B2 (ClosedBookSolvability):} 45 fails (30.8\% of items). The most impactful defect class: nearly a third of the pilot is solvable from world knowledge alone without consulting the supporting fact. These items are not discarded; they are moved to a \texttt{cb\_reserve} pool and used in a separate evaluation slice (Section~\ref{sec:eval}) where pure recall is the explicit target.
  \item \textbf{A4 (TemplateFingerprint):} 9 of 19 templates flagged (47.4\%). The held-out POS-bigram classifier reaches AUC 0.84 on distinguishing template-generated from human-written questions. Templates exceeding the threshold are sent to a paraphrase pass.
  \item \textbf{A3 (FactEcho):} 6 fails (4.1\%) and 59 warns. Verbatim source copying is rare but not absent; flagged items are returned to generation with a stronger paraphrase prompt.
  \item \textbf{D3 (SkewAudit):} maximum country over-representation ratio of $5.84\times$ relative to a uniform baseline at $n=146$. We expect this to attenuate sharply when the corpus reaches the 5{,}000-question target, but we report it explicitly because uneven domain coverage at small $N$ otherwise inflates per-region accuracy estimates.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Go / no-go gating}
