\begin{abstract}
We introduce \textbf{OenoBench}, a knowledge benchmark for the wine domain
comprising \textbf{3{,}266 multiple-choice questions} across six domain
pillars (regions, grape varieties, viticulture, winemaking, producers,
business) and four difficulty tiers. The benchmark is built from
\textbf{38{,}104 atomic, source-anchored facts} extracted by 35
provenance-verified scrapers from authoritative sources spanning
government registries (INAO, TTB, OIV), peer-reviewed journals, university
research groups, and Wikipedia/Wikidata. Our central methodological
contribution is a \emph{LLM-driven, end-to-end pipeline} in which large
language models reformat verified facts into questions and audit the
result, but never serve as the source of truth: every claim traces to a
URL with a tier-of-authority label, every question is generated by one of
five complementary strategies (fact-to-question, template, comparative,
scenario, distractor mining) distributed across five generator models
(Claude, GPT, Gemini, Llama, Qwen + deterministic templates), and every
question is scored by a nine-agent automated audit organised into four
teams and calibrated against a human gold sheet via Cohen's $\kappa$. We
evaluate sixteen frontier configurations and report five findings: (i)
overall accuracy ranges from 53\% to 84\%, with reasoning-mode \textbf{o3}
on top at \textbf{83.6\%}; (ii) reasoning-mode lift is concentrated in
\textbf{DeepSeek R1 (+6.8 pp)} and largely absent for Claude Opus and
Gemini Pro; (iii) the Anthropic family shows a \textbf{+9 pp
self-preference} effect on its own questions while Google models show a
\textbf{$-8$ pp inverse} effect; (iv) frontier open-weight models occupy
the cost-vs-accuracy Pareto frontier together with proprietary reasoning
models; and (v) every config gains \textbf{$\approx$33 pp} on questions
that the audit's closed-book panel solves from world knowledge alone,
revealing a parametric-recall ceiling that only the contextual,
source-grounded slice avoids. We release the corpus, the audit findings,
the human-review web app, and the construction code under CC-BY-SA-4.0.
\end{abstract}
