\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

We evaluate \textbf{16 frontier configurations} on the 3{,}266-question
release in a single end-to-end run. The slate combines six within-family
cost pairs (Claude Opus 4.7 vs.\ Claude Haiku 4.5, Gemini 2.5 Pro vs.\
Gemini 2.5 Flash, GPT-5 vs.\ GPT-5-mini, Llama 3.3 70B vs.\ Llama 3.1
8B, Qwen 2.5 72B vs.\ Qwen 2.5 7B), four reasoning-mode configurations
(o3, DeepSeek R1, Gemini 2.5 Pro thinking, Claude Opus 4.7 thinking),
and two additional standard models (DeepSeek V3, Mistral Large). Every
config sees the same 3{,}266 questions, with single-letter output
(A--D) at \texttt{max\_tokens=5} and a five-stop fallback. We organise
the evaluation around five questions, one per subsection.

\subsection{Overall capability ranking}
\label{sec:eval-rank}

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.86\linewidth]{figures/fig_leaderboard.pdf}
  \caption{Overall accuracy on the 3{,}266-question corpus across 16
    configurations. Reasoning-mode configs are hatched.}
  \label{fig:leaderboard}
\end{figure}

OenoBench cleanly stratifies the slate (Figure~\ref{fig:leaderboard}).
The top tier is led by \textbf{o3 at 83.6\%}, followed by GPT-5
(82.8\%), Gemini 2.5 Pro thinking (82.6\%), Gemini 2.5 Pro (81.7\%),
and Claude Opus 4.7 (81.0\%, tied between standard and thinking modes).
A clear band separates frontier ($\geq$80\%) from mid-tier (mid-60s
to high-70s: Mistral Large 69.1\%, Llama 3.3 70B 67.1\%, Qwen 2.5 72B
67.4\%, DeepSeek V3 70.3\%, Gemini 2.5 Flash 75.1\%, GPT-5-mini 78.4\%)
and small-tier (low-50s to low-60s: Llama 3.1 8B 60.5\%, Qwen 2.5 7B
57.0\%, Claude Haiku 4.5 53.3\%). No config performs at random; the
spread between best and worst is 30~pp, comparable to the spread
observed on GPQA \citep{rein2024gpqa} but on a domain corpus
30$\times$ smaller and fully provenance-tagged.
Difficulty-stratified accuracies (Appendix~\ref{app:eval},
Table~\ref{tab:full-difficulty}) confirm clean stratification on
re-labelled difficulty: from 93.6\% (L1) down to \textbf{58.7\% on L4
expert items}, with frontier configs holding 68--71\% on L4 and small
configs at 39--45\%.

\subsection{Reasoning-mode lift}
\label{sec:eval-reasoning}

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.78\linewidth]{figures/fig_reasoning_lift.pdf}
  \caption{Reasoning-mode vs.\ standard accuracy for four families;
    $\delta$ in pp with 95\% bootstrap CI (1{,}000 resamples). Only
    DeepSeek's CI excludes zero.}
  \label{fig:reasoning}
\end{figure}

Reasoning-mode lift is concentrated in one family
(Figure~\ref{fig:reasoning}). \textbf{DeepSeek R1 vs.\ DeepSeek V3:}
$+$6.8~pp (95\% CI [+4.6, +8.8]), the only paired contrast whose CI
excludes zero. Each of \textbf{o3 vs.\ GPT-5} ($+$0.8~pp [-1.0, +2.6]),
\textbf{Gemini 2.5 Pro thinking vs.\ Gemini 2.5 Pro} ($+$0.9~pp [-1.0,
+2.8]), and \textbf{Claude Opus 4.7 thinking vs.\ Claude Opus 4.7}
($-$0.1~pp [-1.9, +1.8]) is statistically indistinguishable from zero.
We attribute the DeepSeek result to DeepSeek V3 leaving substantial
headroom on this domain (70.3\%) which R1's chain-of-thought can
recover; the three frontier cases are likely already near the corpus
ceiling on parametrically-recallable items. Per-difficulty breakdowns
(Appendix~\ref{app:eval}, Table~\ref{tab:reasoning-by-diff}) confirm
this: reasoning helps DeepSeek across all four difficulty tiers, but
the frontier reasoning configs are flat against their standard
counterparts at L1--L3 and gain at most 1--2 pp at L4. The
cost-vs-lift trade-off is unfavourable for reasoning configs in this
domain except DeepSeek (Section~\ref{sec:eval-cost}).

\subsection{Self-preference bias}
\label{sec:eval-sps}

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \begin{minipage}[t]{0.45\linewidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_sps_heatmap.pdf}
  \end{minipage}\hfill
  \begin{minipage}[t]{0.53\linewidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_sps_lollipop.pdf}
  \end{minipage}
  \caption{Self-preference bias. \emph{Left:} cross-family accuracy
    matrix (rows = evaluator, columns = generator). \emph{Right:}
    per-config SPS $\delta = \mathrm{Acc}(\mathrm{own}) -
    \mathrm{Acc}(\mathrm{other})$ with 95\% bootstrap CI.}
  \label{fig:sps}
\end{figure}

We compute the \emph{Self-Preference Score (SPS)} per config $m$ as
$\mathrm{SPS}(m) = \mathrm{Acc}(m \mid Q_{\mathrm{own}}) -
\mathrm{Acc}(m \mid Q_{\mathrm{other}})$ where $Q_{\mathrm{own}}$
collects questions generated by $m$'s family and $Q_{\mathrm{other}}$
its complement. A pipeline that successfully neutralises generator
fingerprints should produce $|\mathrm{SPS}|$ near zero.

The result (Figure~\ref{fig:sps}) is family-dependent.
\textbf{Anthropic} models show a strong positive SPS:
$+$9.1~pp~[+6.1,+12.1] for Claude Opus 4.7, $+$9.7~pp~[+6.5,+13.1] for
Claude Opus 4.7 thinking, $+$10.0~pp~[+5.7,+14.5] for Claude Haiku 4.5.
\textbf{OpenAI} is statistically indistinguishable from zero across o3,
GPT-5, and GPT-5-mini ($-$1.7 to $+$1.7~pp).
\textbf{Google} models show an unexpected \emph{negative} SPS:
$-$6.1~pp~[$-$10.7,$-$1.8] for Gemini 2.5 Pro, $-$8.0~pp~[$-$12.3,$-$3.8]
for Gemini 2.5 Pro thinking, and $-$10.4~pp~[$-$15.3,$-$5.6] for
Gemini 2.5 Flash.
The cross-family matrix (Figure~\ref{fig:sps}, left) traces the
cause: Google-generated questions are uniformly harder for everyone
(46--76\% across rows), while Anthropic-generated questions are
uniformly easier for Anthropic (77.4\%) but typical for others
(65--86\%). We interpret the Anthropic $+$9~pp gap as a residual
stylistic fingerprint that pre-release paraphrase passes did not
neutralise; the Google $-$8~pp gap suggests Google generators produce
questions that are harder \emph{by phrasing}, not by leak. Both
findings are first-class disclosures with the released corpus.

\subsection{Cost efficiency}
\label{sec:eval-cost}

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.86\linewidth]{figures/fig_pareto_cost.pdf}
  \caption{Total OpenRouter cost (log scale) versus accuracy on the
    full 3{,}266-question slate. Family colours match
    Figure~\ref{fig:leaderboard}; the Pareto frontier (red dashed)
    illustrates the cost-vs-quality trade-off.}
  \label{fig:pareto}
\end{figure}

The 16-config slate spans nearly four orders of magnitude in evaluation
cost: Llama 3.1 8B costs \$0.01 for the full run while Gemini 2.5 Pro
costs \$29.47 (Figure~\ref{fig:pareto}). The Pareto frontier consists
of five configs: \textbf{Llama 3.1 8B} (60.5\%~/~\$0.01),
\textbf{Gemini 2.5 Flash} (75.1\%~/~\$0.12), \textbf{GPT-5-mini}
(78.4\%~/~\$2.82), \textbf{Claude Opus 4.7} (81.0\%~/~\$3.35), and
\textbf{o3} (83.6\%~/~\$11.80). Two of the most expensive configs
(Gemini 2.5 Pro at \$29.47 and GPT-5 at \$21.90) are dominated by
cheaper Pareto neighbours --- Gemini 2.5 Pro by o3, and GPT-5 by
Claude Opus 4.7 / o3 / GPT-5-mini --- because their additional spend
buys output tokens, not better wine answers. Reasoning-mode lift
(Section~\ref{sec:eval-reasoning}) translates into a Pareto win only
for DeepSeek R1, which is itself dominated by GPT-5-mini in this
corpus. The overall efficient-accuracy boundary therefore favours
\emph{small proprietary} (GPT-5-mini, Claude Opus 4.7) and one
reasoning model (o3) over either reasoning-everywhere or
scale-everywhere strategies.

\subsection{Closed-book vs.\ source-grounded performance}
\label{sec:eval-cb}

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.78\linewidth]{figures/fig_cb_vs_source.pdf}
  \caption{Per-config accuracy on B2-flagged closed-book-solvable
    questions ($n{=}1{,}601$) vs.\ contextual / source-grounded
    questions ($n{=}1{,}665$); every config gains $+26.6$ to
    $+39.6$~pp on the closed-book slice.}
  \label{fig:cb}
\end{figure}

The audit's B2 agent reported $\sim$83\% closed-book solvability
against the human reviewer's $\sim$12\% (Section~\ref{sec:gold}).
That disagreement is itself a measurement of what frontier LLMs
already \emph{know} about wine. We exploit it by partitioning the
corpus into the 1{,}601 B2-flagged questions and the 1{,}665
contextual / source-grounded questions, and reporting per-config
accuracy on each slice.

The contrast (Figure~\ref{fig:cb}) is the largest single effect we
observe. The mean per-config closed-book minus contextual gap is
\textbf{$+$32.6~pp}, ranging from $+$26.6~pp (GPT-5) to $+$39.6~pp
(DeepSeek V3), every CI excluding zero. Two readings are mutually
consistent: the B2-flagged slice overlaps heavily with parametric
wine knowledge acquired in pre-training, while the contextual slice
is where wine knowledge has to be reasoned through the source fact
--- \textbf{Claude Opus 4.7 and o3 hold $\sim$70\% on the contextual
slice while small models drop to $\approx$45\%}, and the inter-config
spread widens from 27 to 33~pp.

The methodological consequence is that downstream users can evaluate
on the contextual slice if their research question is
reasoning-with-context, or on the union if it is total wine capability.
The contextual slice is the more discriminative test of wine
reasoning, while the B2-flagged slice is a calibrated indicator of
how much apparent ``wine knowledge'' is parametric recall versus
in-context reasoning.
